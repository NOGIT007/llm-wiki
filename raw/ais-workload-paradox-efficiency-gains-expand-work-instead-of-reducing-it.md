> AI tools don't reduce work — they intensify it. Without deliberate intervention, efficiency gains become unsustainable workload creep.

## Most Companies Are Focused on Getting More People to Use AI

The dominant leadership question right now is adoption. How do we get employees to actually use these tools? The promise is compelling: AI handles routine drafts, summaries, and debugging, freeing workers for higher-value tasks.

But few leaders are asking the harder follow-up question: **what happens when adoption succeeds?**

## Efficiency Gains Don't Reduce Work — They Intensify It

An eight-month study of generative AI adoption at a 200-person U.S. tech company found that AI tools consistently **expanded** work rather than contracting it.

- Workers moved faster, took on broader tasks, and extended work into more hours of the day
- The company didn't mandate AI use — employees did more *on their own initiative* because AI made "doing more" feel possible
- Nobody was asked to take on extra work; it happened organically

## Workers Expand Into Tasks That Were Never Theirs

Because AI fills knowledge gaps, workers stepped into responsibilities that previously belonged to others. Product managers started writing code. Researchers took on engineering tasks. People attempted work they would have outsourced or avoided entirely.

This felt empowering — but it quietly **absorbed work that might have justified additional headcount**. Meanwhile, engineers spent growing amounts of time reviewing and correcting AI-assisted work from colleagues who were now "vibe-coding" outside their expertise.

## The Boundary Between Work and Rest Dissolved

AI removed the friction of starting a task. Workers began prompting during lunch, in meetings, while waiting for files to load. Some sent "one last prompt" before stepping away so AI could work while they were gone.

- Typing a prompt felt like chatting, not working
- Work spilled into evenings and early mornings without deliberate intention
- Downtime no longer provided the same recovery

The boundary between work and non-work didn't disappear. It just became **effortless to cross**.

## Multitasking Created a False Sense of Momentum

AI introduced a new rhythm: writing code while AI generated alternatives, running multiple agents in parallel, reviving deferred tasks because AI could "handle them" in the background.

Workers felt they had a "partner" keeping things moving. The reality was continual attention-switching, constant output-checking, and a growing pile of open threads. As one engineer put it: *"You thought you'd work less. But you don't work less. You just work the same amount or even more."*

## This Creates a Self-Reinforcing Cycle That Leaders Miss

AI accelerates tasks → expectations for speed rise → workers rely more on AI → scope widens → work density increases.

Because the extra effort is **voluntary and framed as enjoyable experimentation**, leaders don't see the load building. What looks like a productivity surge in Q1 becomes cognitive fatigue, impaired judgment, and rising error rates by Q3. The short-term win masks a long-term sustainability problem.

## Organizations Need an "AI Practice," Not Just AI Tools

An AI practice is a set of intentional norms and routines that structure how AI is used, when it's appropriate to stop, and how work should — and should not — expand in response to new capability.

Without such practices, the natural tendency of AI-assisted work is not contraction but **intensification**.

## Build in Intentional Pauses Before Decisions Compound

As tasks speed up and boundaries blur, workers need structured moments to regulate tempo. Not to slow work overall, but to prevent the quiet accumulation of overload.

**Example:** Before any major decision is finalized, require one counterargument and one explicit link to organizational goals. This widens the attention field just enough to protect against drift and poor judgment.

## Sequence Work Deliberately Instead of Reacting Continuously

AI generates outputs constantly. Organizations benefit from norms that shape **when** work moves forward, not just how fast.

- Batch non-urgent notifications
- Hold updates until natural breakpoints
- Protect focus windows where workers are shielded from interruptions

Sequencing reduces fragmentation and costly context-switching while maintaining overall throughput.

## Protect Time for Human Connection and Dialogue

AI enables more solo, self-contained work. That's a problem. Creative insight depends on exposure to multiple human viewpoints — something a single AI synthesis cannot provide.

Short check-ins, shared reflection, and structured dialogue interrupt continuous solo engagement with AI tools. They restore perspective and re-anchor work in social context, countering the depleting, individualizing effects of fast AI-mediated work.

## The Question Isn't Whether AI Will Change Work — It's Whether You'll Shape That Change

Our findings are clear: without intention, AI makes it easier to do more but harder to stop. The organizations that treat AI adoption as a workflow design challenge — not just a tool rollout — will be the ones that capture genuine productivity gains without burning through the people who deliver them.
