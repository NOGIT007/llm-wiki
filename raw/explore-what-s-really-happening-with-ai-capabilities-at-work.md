Every AI roadmap I've seen this year treats capability like a light switch -- either AI can do something perfectly or it can't do it at all. The reality is messier and more interesting.

Recent research reveals that AI's infamous "jaggedness" -- being brilliant at coding but hopeless at basic reasoning, or excelling at analysis while failing at simple tasks -- wasn't actually an inherent property of AI systems. It was an artifact of how we were asking them to work. **The frontier is smoothing out, and the implications for how we structure work are profound.**

## The jaggedness was never about the AI

For two years, we've organised our thinking around AI's uneven capabilities. You could get impressive results on complex coding tasks, then watch the same system fail at elementary logic problems. This seemed fundamental to how these systems worked.

New evidence suggests otherwise. When researchers at major AI labs started applying organisational structures to AI work -- breaking tasks into components, adding review cycles, creating feedback loops -- the jaggedness began to disappear. The Cursor coding assistant recently solved a research-grade mathematics problem by using the same systematic approach that had previously built a web browser. Same underlying model, dramatically different results.

The difference wasn't raw intelligence. It was structure.

## What works for human teams works for AI agents

The breakthrough insight is deceptively simple: AI performance improves when you treat AI systems like teams rather than oracles. The most effective implementations now follow the same pattern that drives human productivity: decompose the work, parallelise execution, verify outputs, iterate toward completion.

Consider how a software development team tackles a complex feature. Product managers break down requirements, engineers work on components simultaneously, code reviews catch errors, and the team iterates based on testing. When AI researchers applied this same framework to AI agents -- giving them structured environments, decomposed tasks, and verification processes -- performance jumped across the board.

The "harness" matters as much as the engine.

## Two types of work are emerging as AI-ready

Not everything can be decomposed and verified easily, but a clear pattern is emerging around what can. **Work falls into two tiers of verifiability, and both are increasingly within AI's reach.**

Tier 1 covers machine-checkable tasks: code that compiles or fails, tests that pass or break, calculations that produce verifiable outputs. These were the early wins, and they're getting more sophisticated as AI systems gain better tools and longer thinking time.

Tier 2 is more interesting: expert-checkable work with clear criteria. Mathematical proofs, engineering designs, legal briefs, financial analyses -- tasks where a human expert can quickly verify correctness even if they couldn't produce the work as quickly themselves. The key insight is that verification often requires less expertise than creation.

If your work can be decomposed into verifiable sub-problems, it's probably AI-ready. If it can't, that may be temporary.

## The skill that matters most isn't what you think

The most valuable human skill in this environment isn't prompt engineering or AI literacy. It's the ability to structure problems so they can be delegated and verified. Call it meta-skill: knowing how to break down complex work, recognising what good output looks like, and building systems that catch errors.

This mirrors what happened in software development over the past decade. The most effective engineers aren't necessarily the fastest coders -- they're the ones who can architect maintainable systems, review others' work effectively, and structure problems so teams can solve them in parallel. The same dynamic is playing out with AI.

Your competitive advantage increasingly comes from what you can orchestrate, not what you can execute personally. Even individual "geniuses" rely on the collective knowledge and structures of their fields.

## Stop asking the wrong question

Most organisations are still asking "Can AI do this specific task?" when they should be asking "Can this work be decomposed into verifiable sub-problems?" The first question leads to pilot projects that never scale. The second leads to systematic transformation.

The evidence suggests we're moving toward a world where AI handles an increasing share of decomposable, verifiable work -- which turns out to be more work than most people expected. But this only happens when you build the right structures around it.

**Here's what to do this week:**

1. **Audit your hardest tasks** for decomposability. Pick one complex project and break it into smaller, verifiable components. If you can do this clearly, AI can probably help.

2. **Develop your verification instincts.** Practice "sniff-checking" work in your domain -- quickly assessing whether output meets quality standards without redoing the entire task.

3. **Experiment with AI harnesses.** Give AI systems structured environments to work in: templates, checklists, clear success criteria. Treat them like capable team members who need context, not magic boxes that should divine your intent.

4. **Focus on meta-skills.** Invest in learning how to structure problems, delegate effectively, and build verification processes. These skills will matter more than domain expertise in many contexts.

The frontier is smoothing faster than most organisations realise. The question isn't whether AI can do complex work -- it's whether you can structure complex work for AI to do.
