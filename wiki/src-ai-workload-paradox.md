## Summary

An eight-month study of generative AI adoption at a 200-person U.S. tech company reveals that AI tools consistently expand work rather than reducing it. Without deliberate intervention, efficiency gains become unsustainable workload creep. This provides empirical evidence for [[Jevons Paradox]] applied to knowledge work.

## Key Findings

- **Efficiency intensifies work** -- Workers moved faster, took on broader tasks, and extended work into more hours. Nobody was asked to do more; it happened organically because AI made "doing more" feel possible.

- **Role boundary dissolution** -- AI fills knowledge gaps, so workers stepped into others' responsibilities. Product managers started writing code. Researchers took on engineering tasks. This quietly absorbed work that might have justified additional headcount.

- **Work-rest boundary collapse** -- AI removed the friction of starting a task. Workers began prompting during lunch, in meetings, before stepping away. Typing a prompt felt like chatting, not working.

- **False momentum from multitasking** -- Running multiple agents in parallel created a sense of productivity that masked continual attention-switching, constant output-checking, and growing open threads.

- **Self-reinforcing cycle** -- AI accelerates tasks, speed expectations rise, workers rely more on AI, scope widens, work density increases. Because extra effort is voluntary and framed as enjoyable experimentation, leaders miss the load building. Q1 productivity surges become Q3 cognitive fatigue.

## Recommended Interventions

The article argues organizations need an "AI practice" (intentional norms and routines), not just AI tools:
- Build intentional pauses before decisions compound
- Sequence work deliberately instead of reacting continuously
- Protect time for human connection and dialogue
