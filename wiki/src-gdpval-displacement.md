## Key Thesis

[[OpenAI]]'s [[GDPval]] benchmark measures not what AI can do but what it already does -- better, faster, and cheaper than humans in real professional work. The 70.9% displacement figure represents an economic wrecking ball for professional services, and the trajectory points to 90-100% by December 2026.

## Important Claims

[[GDPval]] is an economic displacement benchmark, not a capability test. It evaluates 44 occupations using real client requests with blinded expert evaluation across financial reconciliation, legal document review, marketing copy, data analysis, technical writing, and strategic recommendations.

**The breakdown of 70.9%:**
- 40-50% of cases: AI output beats human work (blinded experts preferred it)
- 20-30% of cases: AI output is indistinguishable from human work
- 30% of cases: AI produces a capable junior-associate-level draft needing review

**Cost implications are an order of magnitude:** Financial reconciliation drops from $375 (5 hrs) to $43 (30 min) -- 89% reduction. Legal document review: $800 to $120 -- 85%. Marketing briefs: $600 to $75 -- 87%.

The trajectory matters more than the current number. December 2025: 70%. Projected: 75% by March 2026, 80% by June, 90-100% by December 2026. Human parity (50%) was passed months ago.

The source argues that average professional work has lost its economic moat. The premium moves to the edges: taste and judgment that cannot be verified, relationships and trust that cannot be automated, novel combinations absent from training data, and accountability clients pay for regardless of quality. Everything in the middle becomes [[Intelligence Commoditization]] -- "commodity intelligence."

The SaaS business model faces disruption: per-seat pricing assumes human users, but when companies can replace junior staff with AI, the "user" becomes an API consumer. Horizontal tools commoditize; vertical solutions differentiate; infrastructure develops monopoly dynamics.
