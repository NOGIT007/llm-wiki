## Key Thesis

AI's notorious "jaggedness" -- brilliant at coding, hopeless at basic reasoning -- was never an inherent property of AI systems. It was an artifact of how we asked them to work. When researchers applied organizational structures (task decomposition, review cycles, feedback loops), the [[Jagged Frontier]] began to disappear. [[Work Structure]] determines AI capability, not raw intelligence.

## Important Claims

The breakthrough insight: AI performance improves dramatically when systems are treated like teams rather than oracles. The most effective pattern mirrors human productivity -- decompose work, parallelize execution, verify outputs, iterate toward completion. The "harness" matters as much as the engine. The Cursor coding assistant solved a research-grade math problem using the same systematic approach that had previously built a web browser -- same model, different structure, dramatically different results.

**Two tiers of AI-ready work are emerging:**

- **Tier 1 (machine-checkable)**: Code that compiles or fails, tests that pass or break, calculations with verifiable outputs. These were early wins, growing more sophisticated.
- **Tier 2 (expert-checkable)**: Mathematical proofs, engineering designs, legal briefs, financial analyses -- work where a human expert can quickly verify correctness even if they could not produce it as fast. Key insight: [[Verification]] often requires less expertise than creation.

The most valuable human skill is **meta-skill**: the ability to structure problems so they can be delegated and verified. This mirrors the evolution in software engineering where the best engineers architect systems and review work rather than writing the most code. Competitive advantage comes from what you can orchestrate, not execute personally.

Organizations should stop asking "Can AI do this task?" and start asking "Can this work be decomposed into verifiable sub-problems?" The first leads to stalled pilots; the second leads to systematic transformation.
