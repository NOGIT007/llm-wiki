# Jevons Paradox

When efficiency improvements make a resource cheaper to use, total consumption of that resource rises rather than falls. Named after William Stanley Jevons, who observed in 1865 that more efficient steam engines increased coal consumption — because cheaper energy unlocked new applications that hadn't been economical before.

## Applied to AI

The parallel to artificial intelligence is direct: as intelligence becomes cheap (cost down 99.8% in four years), organizations don't simply do the same work faster — they discover entirely new categories of work that were previously too expensive to attempt.

This means:

- **Cheap intelligence creates more demand for human judgment, not less.** Every AI-generated draft needs review. Every automated workflow needs design and monitoring.
- **Efficiency gains expand workload.** Teams that adopt AI often report doing more total work, not less — the "workload paradox."
- **The bottleneck shifts.** When generation is free, the scarce resource becomes verification, taste, and strategic direction.

## Why It Matters

Organizations expecting AI to simply reduce headcount are applying a linear mental model to a nonlinear phenomenon. The historical pattern — steam engines, electricity, computing — consistently shows that making a capability cheap makes it ubiquitous, not redundant.
