## Summary

This article applies [[Jevons Paradox]] to AI and cognitive labor. The central thesis: AI does not eliminate jobs by making cognitive work efficient -- it makes intelligence cheaper, driving exponential new demand for human judgment and problem-solving. The fear of mass unemployment rests on a fixed-demand fallacy.

## Core Argument

In 1865, more efficient steam engines were expected to reduce coal consumption. Instead, cheaper coal usage unlocked entirely new applications (factories, trains, ships), and total consumption skyrocketed. AI applies this same paradox to cognitive work via [[Intelligence Commoditization]].

## Key Claims

- **The fixed-demand fallacy** -- The economy does not require a fixed amount of code, text, or analysis. Economic demand is highly elastic.
- **Cheaper software unlocks niche markets** -- Custom software was previously restricted to large organizations. As AI makes coding cheaper, small businesses gain bespoke tools and individual users get hyper-personalized micro-apps.
- **Creative value shifts from execution to direction** -- Just as photography democratized image-making without destroying visual art, AI lowers production barriers while shifting premium value to creative direction and strategy.
- **Professional services scale to excluded populations** -- Cheaper diagnostics and legal administration make services accessible to millions who previously could not afford them, creating massive new markets.
- **Human labor moves up the value chain** -- AI absorbs repetitive drudge work. Deciding what work to do becomes the most valuable skill.
